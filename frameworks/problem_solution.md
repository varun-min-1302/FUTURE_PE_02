# Framework 1: Problem → Solution

## Overview

The Problem-Solution framework is the most fundamental and widely-used UGC ad 
structure. It mirrors how people naturally recommend products in real life: 
"I had this problem, I found this thing, it fixed it."

This framework works because it activates a psychological pattern called 
**pain-relief motivation** — viewers who share the same problem immediately 
pay attention because their brain is wired to seek resolution.

---

## Structure Breakdown

### Step 1: HOOK (0–3 seconds)
**Purpose:** Stop the scroll by calling out a specific, relatable problem.

The hook must make the viewer feel *seen*. It should describe a frustration 
so specific that the target audience thinks, "Wait, that's literally me."

**What works:**
- First-person frustration statements ("I was SO tired of...")
- Direct address to the viewer ("If your skin does THIS, watch this")
- Hyperbolic but honest exaggeration ("My face was basically an oil slick by noon")

**What doesn't work:**
- Vague or generic openings ("Skincare is hard")
- Starting with the product name or brand
- Overly dramatic claims that break trust

**Example for oily skin serum:**
> "I literally used to blot my face THREE times before lunch — my oily skin 
> was out of control."

---

### Step 2: PROBLEM AMPLIFICATION (3–8 seconds)
**Purpose:** Deepen the emotional connection by showing the *consequences* 
of the problem, not just the problem itself.

This is where you go beyond the surface-level issue and tap into how the 
problem makes the person *feel* — self-conscious, frustrated, exhausted 
from trying solutions that don't work.

**Techniques:**
- List failed solutions ("I tried mattifying creams, powders, setting sprays — nothing lasted")
- Show emotional impact ("I dreaded video calls because my skin looked so greasy")
- Quantify the struggle ("I've wasted over ₹5,000 on products that didn't work")

**Example:**
> "I tried mattifying creams, powders, setting sprays — nothing lasted past lunch. 
> I was spending money every month on things that just... didn't work."

---

### Step 3: SOLUTION INTRODUCTION (8–15 seconds)
**Purpose:** Introduce the product as the natural answer — NOT as a sales pitch.

The transition from problem to solution must feel organic. The product should 
enter the narrative the way a friend's recommendation would — casually, 
with a sense of genuine discovery.

**Natural transition phrases:**
- "Then I started using..."
- "A friend recommended..."
- "I saw this on Reddit and figured why not..."
- "My dermatologist actually suggested..."

**Key rules:**
- Mention the product name only ONCE in this section
- State 1-2 key features as benefits (not specs)
- Show the product being used, not just held up

**Example:**
> "Then I started using Minimalist Niacinamide serum. Just two drops, every 
> morning. It's lightweight, not sticky — absorbs in like 10 seconds."

---

### Step 4: PROOF / RESULTS (15–22 seconds)
**Purpose:** Provide tangible evidence that the solution actually worked.

This is where credibility is built. The proof can be visual (showing skin), 
verbal (describing timeline of results), or social (mentioning others who 
noticed the difference).

**Types of proof:**
- **Visual proof:** Before/after shots, close-up of skin, no-filter footage
- **Timeline proof:** "After 2 weeks, I noticed..." "By week 4..."
- **Third-party validation:** "My mom actually asked what I changed"
- **Behavioral proof:** "I stopped using concealer" / "I skip setting spray now"

**Example:**
> "It's been a month and honestly? My skin stays matte till evening. 
> No touch-ups. My roommate asked me what I changed."

---

### Step 5: CTA (22–25 seconds)
**Purpose:** Tell the viewer exactly what to do next, with a reason to act now.

The CTA should feel like the natural end of a conversation, not a hard sell. 
It should be one sentence, maximum two.

**Example:**
> "Link's in the bio. Your oily skin era is over."

---

## When to Use This Framework

| Scenario | Effectiveness |
|---|---|
| Product solves a clear, common pain point | ★★★★★ |
| Target audience is problem-aware | ★★★★★ |
| New product launch | ★★★★☆ |
| Competitive market (need differentiation) | ★★★☆☆ |
| Luxury/aspirational products | ★★☆☆☆ |

**Best for:** Skincare, health supplements, fitness products, productivity tools, 
household problem-solvers — anything where the audience is actively seeking 
a solution to a known frustration.

## Visual Direction Template

| Timestamp | Visual | Audio/Text |
|---|---|---|
| 0–3s | Creator talking to camera, frustrated expression | Hook line spoken naturally |
| 3–8s | Shows failed products OR gestures to problem area | Problem amplification |
| 8–15s | Picks up product, applies it, shows texture | Solution introduction |
| 15–22s | Close-up of results, confident expression | Proof / results |
| 22–25s | Looks at camera, smiles, points down | CTA + text overlay with link |

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Starting with the product instead of the problem.** The viewer doesn't 
   care about your product yet — they care about their own pain.

2. **Being too vague with the problem.** "Bad skin" is weak. "My foundation 
   separating by 2pm and my pores showing through" is strong.

3. **Making the solution transition feel like a hard cut to an ad.** The shift 
   from problem to product should feel like the next sentence in a story, 
   not a commercial break.

4. **Skipping the proof section.** Without evidence, the whole script is just 
   a claim. Proof is what converts skeptics into buyers.

5. **Weak CTA.** "Check it out if you want" is passive. "Link in bio — your 
   oily skin era is over" is directive and memorable.
